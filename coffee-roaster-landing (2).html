Why This Matters

Most roasters discover permit requirements after they've already bought the equipment.

You find the perfect space. You order a beautiful 15kg roaster. The building inspector asks for your air permit. You call the air district. They tell you the permit takes 3–6 months. Your landlord is charging rent. Your equipment is sitting in crates. Your opening date just moved to next quarter.

Or worse: you start roasting without a permit, hoping no one notices. The district eventually catches up with you. Now you're applying for a permit after installation—which means your equipment gets evaluated against today's emission standards, not the standards that existed when you bought it.

The air permit must be issued before installation or delivery to stay compliant. Starting operations early can result in violations, penalties, and potential permit denial. SCAQMD is currently evaluating permit denials for facilities that bypassed pre-construction review.

Source: SCAQMD Permit Streamlining Task Force, December 2025

This isn't theoretical. We've seen it happen to roasters who did everything else right—found a great location, invested in quality equipment, built a loyal customer base—only to face months of delays and thousands in unexpected costs because they didn't understand California's air permitting requirements upfront.

That's why we created this toolkit.
